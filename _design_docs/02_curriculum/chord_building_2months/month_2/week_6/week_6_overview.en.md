# Week 6 — Adding color: shimmer with the 9th tension
> Curriculum: chord_building_2months · Month 2 · Parent: ../month_2_overview.md · dayKey: m2.w6

So far you built chords from R·3·5·7. This week you lay **one more note on top to make the color shimmer.** That note is the **9th.** Don't be scared — the 9th isn't hard jazz theory; it's just **"the root's major 2nd, raised one octave"** (C's 9th = D). Reach one more finger onto the m7·Maj7 you already hold and you get the sophisticated colors **m9·Maj9.** Simple formula, pro sound.

## This week's goal
Over a 5th-string root, add a **9th** to m7·Maj7·7 to make m9·Maj9·9, and always know where the 9th sits (usually on top). The finish line is hearing the **color difference** by alternating the plain chord and the 9-chord. (The 9th renders in **yellow** — it's a color tone.)

## This week's journey
- **Day 1** — What is the 9th? How to find it from the root. Maj7 → **Maj9.**
- **Day 2** — m7 → **m9.** The finger path to lay the 9th onto the m7 you know.
- **Day 3** — 7 (dominant) → **9.** The shimmer when the tension comes on top. Contrast plain 7 vs 9.
- **Day 4** — Weekly integration. Cycle Maj9 → 9 → m9 over one root. The 9th stays on top; only the skeleton changes.

## Core feel
- **9th = root + major 2nd (one octave up).** One note, one formula.
- **The 9th is a color tone (yellow).** If R·3·7 are the skeleton (blue), the 9th is the color sprinkled on top.
- Still **intervals** — the 3rd & 7th set the character, the 9th paints the color.
