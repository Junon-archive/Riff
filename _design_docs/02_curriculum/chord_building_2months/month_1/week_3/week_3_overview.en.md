# Week 3 — Mixing 6th/5th roots to connect ii–V–I by the shortest path (voice leading)
> Curriculum: chord_building_2months · Month 1 · Parent: ../month_1_overview.md · dayKey: m1.w3

Week 1 was the 6th-string root, Week 2 the 5th-string root, learned separately. This week you **mix them.** When you play the heart of popular music — the **ii–V–I** (e.g., Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7 in C) — you don't jump your hand across the neck; you **choose the root string (6th or 5th)** so the three chords connect almost in place. This is what pros call **voice leading** — the skill of letting notes slide the shortest distance when chords change.

## This week's goal
Cycle the C-key ii–V–I as **Dm7 (5th) → G7 (6th) → Cmaj7 (5th)** smoothly, minimizing hand movement. The finish line: knowing every time whether each root is on the 6th or 5th string, and seeing/hearing the guide tones (3rds and 7ths) move only a half step into the next chord.

## This week's journey
- **Day 1** — What ii–V–I is and why it's pop's heart. "Why do chords change without going far?" = the root-string-crossing idea.
- **Day 2** — Connect Dm7 (5th)-G7 (6th)-Cmaj7 (5th) in one fret region. The three root-string differences + minimal finger movement.
- **Day 3** — The same pattern in another key (D key: Em7-A7-Dmaj7). Absorb that ii–V–I is a movable pattern that slides as a whole.
- **Day 4** — Weekly integration. Cycle ii–V–I over a backing track with minimal hand movement; complete the "no jumping" feel.

## Core feel
- **Choose the root string to tie your hand to one region.** Cross 6th-string and 5th-string chords and you never run up the neck.
- **The guide tones (3rd & 7th) move only a half step.** When chords change, common tones stay, the rest barely shifts.
- Amateurs change chords in "blocks"; pros connect to the **nearest note.**
