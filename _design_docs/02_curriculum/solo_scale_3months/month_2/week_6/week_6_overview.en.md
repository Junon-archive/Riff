# Week 6 — Same Hands, Just Moving Your Mind from Building A to Building C
> Curriculum: solo_scale_3months · Month 2 · Parent: ../month_2_overview.md · dayKey: m2.w6

Last week you chewed through all 7 notes of A natural minor, right? Here's a bit of a surprising fact: that exact fretboard shape, that exact fingering — without changing a single finger — you can also use it as **C major** starting this week. That's because A minor and C major are "relative keys" that share the exact same 7 notes (A-B-C-D-E-F-G). The notes are completely identical; the only thing that changes is **which note feels like "home."** This week isn't about memorizing a new scale — it's about building the mindset of reading a fretboard you already know in two different keys.

## What We're Doing This Week
Using relative keys, you'll get a hands-on feel for the "same notes, different center" relationship between **A natural minor and C major**. This week, you'll reread the 7 notes you completed last week through a C major lens, building the ability to move freely between minor's dark landing (C) and **major's bright 3rd landing (E)**. Think of it like the same apartment complex — if you head home to Building A, that's A minor; if you head home to Building C, that's C major. The building never changes, only the home address does.

## Why We're Doing This Now
In W5, you got the 7 notes of A natural minor into your hands. But those 7 notes are **completely identical** to C major — the only difference is the center tone (the resolution point). So instead of hunting for a new shape, you get two keys for free just by changing "where you let the fretboard you already know flow to." This "one fretboard, two keys" mindset carries straight through to next week's chord-tone mapping. If you get the bright 3rd, E, into your hands here, W7 gets a lot easier.

## This Week's Journey (Day 1–4)
- **Day 1 · Engrave Both Home Addresses in Your Fingertips** — Read the same fretboard in two keys. Write degrees paired as `(C major / A minor)`, so you learn by eye and by voice that the same note C is "the ♭3 in Am, the root in C major." Never change the shape — all you're moving is your ear and your landing point.
- **Day 2 · Plant the Bright 3rd, E** — 99% of C major's identity is its bright 3rd, **E**. Hop through 3rd intervals and you'll land on a chord tone almost every time — C→E, D→F, E→G. Especially make the C→E jump feel like a memorized melody in your hand, and land on E by holding it out long. Watch out for accidentally landing on F (the 4th) right next to E.
- **Day 3 · Bounce Only Between the Landing Points C and E** — Real songs go back and forth between Am and C constantly. Roll continuously in 4-note sequences, keeping the hand path identical, and switch only the **final landing note** — C for Am, E for C. Notice how the note positions in two bars are nearly identical, yet a single landing note flips the whole brightness.
- **Day 4 · Real-World Switching Jam** — Skip the drills and let this loose as a free jam. Over an Am↔C backing track, change your solo's color every time the chord changes. E is C's 3rd and also Am's 5th; C is Am's ♭3 and also C's root — it's a safe zone shared by both chords, so you can't go wrong wherever you land in that pocket. Keep the landing fixed, and change up the path in between every time.

## This Week's Core Sense
Same 7 notes, **two resolution tones** — C (♭3) in Am, E (the 3rd) in C major. This week's new target is "the bright 3rd, E." The whole game is reflexes: switching your landing note **without lagging even half a beat** the instant the chord changes. If you wait to hear the chord before deciding your landing point, you're already late. Memorize the backing track's progression ahead of time, and have your next landing note ready a full beat early. The rule stays the same: no simple up-and-down runs — approach everything with 3rd intervals and 4-note sequences only.

## By the End of This Week
- Read one fretboard in two keys, A minor and C major, recognizing each note's degree under both names.
- Instantly switch your landing note between C and E whenever the backing track's chord section changes.
- In an Am↔C switching jam, tell apart the bright/dark difference of "same material, different center" by ear, playing a solo that fills in differently each time instead of copying the same lick.
- Compare your Day 1 recording to your Day 4 recording, and confirm for yourself that a first door has opened — "singing on your own, on the fretboard."
