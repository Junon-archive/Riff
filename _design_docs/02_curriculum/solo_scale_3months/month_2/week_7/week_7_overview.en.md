# Week 7 — Where's the Skeleton of the Chord You're Hearing Right Now, Hiding on the Neck?
> Curriculum: solo_scale_3months · Month 2 · Parent: ../month_2_overview.md · dayKey: m2.w7

Time for a bit of a confession. Six weeks in, the pentatonic box feels comfortable in your hands, but if your improv still feels like it's "only playing inside the box," there's exactly one reason: **you don't know where the actual skeleton notes of the chord you're hearing right now sit on the neck**. This week, we're pulling just the "core skeleton" — chord tones — out of the broad "surface" that is a scale. This isn't speed training. It's **brain training**: recognizing positions, going "oh, I pressed here and it's C, and here too, it's C." That's why this week's BPM stays very slow the whole way through. Speed has never once been the main character in this curriculum.

## What We're Doing This Week
We'll map the **1st, 3rd, and 5th chord tones (arpeggio)** of our target chords (Am, C) across the whole neck. For Am that's A-C-E; for C, it's C-E-G. These three notes are safe — they fit beautifully wherever you press them while that chord is ringing. The problem is we only know them in one spot (Box 1). So we'll scatter them across the whole neck, from the 6th string to the 1st, and draw a "mental map you can find with your eyes closed."

## Why We're Doing This Now
In W6, you gained the mindset of reading one fretboard in two keys. Now it's time to pull out just the chord tones on top of that. The notes you can safely land on in improv are, in the end, chord tones — and among those, **the 3rd is what decides the color**. What makes Am sound minor is C; what makes C sound bright and major is E. This chord-tone map becomes the raw material for next week's ii-V-I guide-tone line. Master "moving to the nearest 3rd" here, and W8 becomes a breeze.

## This Week's Journey (Day 1–4)
- **Day 1 · Draw the Am Map** — Map Am chord tones (A-C-E) across the whole neck, and mark the 3rd, **C (♭3)**, in red. Run up and down the 1-3-5 arpeggio from the 5th-fret position (BPM 66), aiming not for speed but for calling out the degree of every note in your head. Improvise using only A, C, and E — and always stop on C.
- **Day 2 · Draw the C Map** — Map the chord tones (C-E-G) of the partner chord, C major, this time targeting the 3rd, **E**. Here's something to notice: E, yesterday's 5th of Am, is today's 3rd of C; C, yesterday's ♭3 of Am, is today's root of C. Look at it as "same note, different role," and you already know half the map.
- **Day 3 · Nudge from 3rd to 3rd** — Am's 3rd is C; C's 3rd is E. Moving from Am to C, there's exactly one move to aim for: **C → E**. And on the fretboard, this turns out to be absurdly close — 3rd string 5th fret (C) to 2nd string 5th fret (E), just one finger over on the same fret, one string away. Engrave the thrill of "minimal movement" — the chord changed, but your hand barely moved.
- **Day 4 · Sing Using Only Chord Tones, and Grade Yourself with a Recording** — No new concepts today; this is where you prove the week. Over an Am-C backing track, build something solo-like using only chord tones, record it, and grade yourself against exactly one standard: **did you land properly on the 3rd every time the chord changed?** All the Am and C chord tones sit right inside the 5th-8th fret home-base box, so your hand should feel right at home.

## This Week's Core Sense
1, 3, and 5 are safe chord tones, and among them, **the 3rd (C in Am, E in C)** is your landing target. When the chord changes, you're not re-running the scale — you're moving your hand to the "nearest 3rd." That sense of connecting 3rd to 3rd with minimal movement is everything this week. And a good solo isn't about playing lots of notes — it's about **sitting well**. Two or three notes per chord, then a light vibrato on the 3rd at the end. That restraint is exactly what sounds professional, and it's the seed for next month's phrasing.

## By the End of This Week
- Find the Am (A-C-E) and C (C-E-G) arpeggios with your eyes closed across multiple positions on the neck.
- Connect the two 3rds (C and E) with a minimal, right-next-door move whenever the chord switches between Am and C.
- Run a chord-tone-only jam for a full minute without breaking, then slow the recording down to grade your own 3rd landings, timing, and note endings.
