# Week 8 — While the Chord Changes Three Times, Just Three Notes, Exactly Right
> Curriculum: solo_scale_3months · Month 2 · Parent: ../month_2_overview.md · dayKey: m2.w8

When the chords whip past on a backing track, your hand freezes up, right? You're thinking "wait, what chord is this, where do I even put my finger," and by the time you figure it out, you've missed the beat entirely. The reason is that when the chord changes, **you're trying to keep track of all five notes at once**. Your brain just can't keep up. So here's a cheat code jazz players have been using for a hundred years: **guide tones** — grabbing onto just one note, the **3rd**, of each chord. This week, we use that cheat code to conquer the famous **ii-V-I** progression, the one that shows up everywhere in jazz, pop, and ballads, and wrap up Month 2 with it.

## What We're Doing This Week
Over a **ii-V-I (in the key of C: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7)** backing track, you'll land precisely on the **3rd (guide tone)** of each chord every time it changes. There's no need to memorize a whole scale — just three notes.

- **Dm7's 3rd = F**
- **G7's 3rd = B**
- **Cmaj7's 3rd = E**

In other words, **F → B → E**. Land just these three notes on the beat, and people will say "this person actually knows the changes." The root exists in every chord so it's kind of flat, and the 5th tends to overlap too — but the 3rd is the chord's "expression."

## Why We're Doing This Now
In W7, you learned the chord-tone map and how to "move to the nearest 3rd." Now it's time to apply that to ii-V-I, the actual backbone of real music. Here's the heart of this week — **G7's B wants to flow into the next chord's E, Cmaj7's 3rd.** That's the feeling of tension (G7) resolving into release (Cmaj7). And there's one more piece of fretboard magic: in the "home" position, **F (4th string 3rd fret) and E (4th string 2nd fret) are just one fret apart on the same string** — a half step. Moving from G7 to Cmaj7, all you have to do is slide F down to E. This is the prettiest voice movement in jazz — the F→E half-step resolution.

## This Week's Journey (Day 1–4)
- **Day 1 · Get the Three Notes' Locations Into Your Hand First** — All three chords' roots and 3rds gather right in the "home" position (frets 2-4). F is 4th string 3rd fret, B is 3rd string 4th fret, E is 4th string 2nd fret. Scatter in the other F, B, and E positions around the neck too. Today isn't about rote memorization — just build the reflex "chord change = press the 3rd." Always name the note out loud (F, B, E) as you fret it.
- **Day 2 · Connect the Three Dots Into One Line** — Fret the three notes separately as isolated "dots" and it sounds like a "fretting test." Today, build a **guide-tone line** using 3rd intervals and short connecting notes. The 3rd lands on the first beat of each bar (the chord change), and you'll engrave the F→E half-step resolution and the B→E leading tone into your hands and ears at the same time. "Skeleton is F-B-E, flesh is free" — that's the improv rule.
- **Day 3 · It's Not "What," It's "When"** — Lots of people fret the right note and still sound wrong. That's because the timing is off. Today's goal is one thing only: **make the 3rd ring exactly on the downbeat where the chord changes.** The secret is preparing ahead (resting your finger on the next 3rd before the change happens) and not being afraid of space. You'll also work through a fast ii-V where each chord only gets two beats.
- **Day 4 · Operate With Your Ears** — No new technique today; record this week's result and grade your own playing against three standards: (1) landing timing, (2) whether the B→E resolution is audible, (3) the final vibrato on E. Slow the playback down to 0.75x and 0.5x, and half-beat errors that were invisible in real time show up under a microscope. Whatever improvement you spot here becomes your personal homework before moving into Month 3.

## This Week's Core Sense
It's about connecting the three guide tones — **F (Dm7's 3rd) → B (G7's 3rd) → E (Cmaj7's 3rd)** — with minimal movement. The key isn't "what," it's "when": the 3rd has to arrive exactly on the downbeat where the chord changes. Rest your finger on the next 3rd ahead of time, and after you land, don't be afraid of space. Rather than cramming in notes and losing all your timing, **three accurate landings beat thirty misaligned notes, a hundred times over.** If things speed up and you panic, throw out every connecting note and just fret the three 3rds on the beat — the music will still stand.

## By the End of This Week
- Instantly find the fretboard positions of the guide tones F, B, and E across the Dm7→G7→Cmaj7 progression.
- Land the 3rd right on the downbeat of every chord change, smoothly connecting the line with the F→E half-step resolution and the B→E leading tone.
- Hold your timing even through a fast ii-V (two beats per chord), and know how to use space as a skill.
- Slow your own recording down for analysis, find one misaligned landing yourself, and carry it forward into Month 3.
