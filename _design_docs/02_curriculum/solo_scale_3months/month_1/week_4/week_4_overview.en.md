# Week 4 — Blues (♭5) + Bending/Vibrato Technique + One-Chord Jam
> Curriculum: solo_scale_3months · Month 1 · Parent: ../month_1_overview.md · dayKey: m1.w4

This week is the highlight of Month 1. Over the last three weeks, you've built the whole-neck map and landing on the 3rd — now it's time to paint "the flavor of blues" on top of all that. We expand the pentatonic into a blues scale by adding just one blue note — **♭5** — and finally, formally learn the expressive technique that makes that note actually "speak": **accurate-pitch bending and vibrato.** If you've been "using" bends and vibrato this whole time without ever properly learning them, this week's mini-lesson fills that gap. At the end, you record a whole one-chord jam, give yourself feedback, and wrap up the entire month.

## This Week's Goal
First, locate ♭5 across all 5 boxes and weave it in as a passing tone. Second, treat bending as "landing accurately on the target pitch," distinguishing half (semitone) and full (whole tone) bends and tuning them by ear. Third, apply vibrato evenly using wrist rotation. And this week's finish line is weaving all these ingredients into one blues phrase, then recording a one-chord jam, evaluating yourself, and wrapping up the whole month.

## Why Now (Connecting to Last Week)
In Weeks 1-3, you built the skeleton — all 5 boxes and landing on the 3rd. With that skeleton in place, it's time to add the ingredients that turn "a lineup of scale notes" into "a blues phrase." The order matters here — we learn bending first, then vibrato, because wrist vibrato is essentially "repeating a very tiny bend" — it naturally comes after bending. Expressive technique only becomes real music once you've fully memorized the fretboard; add it any earlier, and it just complicates your hands.

## This Week's Journey
- **Day 1** — Introducing ♭5 plus an accurate-pitch bending mini-lesson. Learn to slip through the ♭5 positions inside Box 1 (5th string 6th fret, 3rd string 8th fret) as passing tones (BPM 70, Am7 backing), then learn bending. The key is **hear the reference first, then match by ear.** Pluck the target note (say, the 5th, E) somewhere else first to plant it in your ear, then bend up to match that exact pitch. Your ears are the judge here, not your eyes. One handy combo: bend the 4th (D) up just a half step and you get ♭5; bend it a full step and you land on the 5th (E). Bending comes from your wrist and forearm rotating, not finger strength — brace with two supporting fingers behind it.
- **Day 2** — A mini-lesson on the vibrato that makes a note sing, plus mapping ♭5 across every box. We distinguish finger vibrato from wrist vibrato, but today's main focus is **wrist vibrato (wide sweep).** With the fret held down, rotate your wrist like turning a doorknob, repeating "up-down" (at quarter- and eighth-note cycles, BPM 70). Then, as you map every ♭5 position across the whole neck, remember that in improv, **you approach ♭5 by "entering into it" with a slide or bend, and every landing note always gets a slow, wide vibrato.**
- **Day 3** — Turning scale into phrase. Melt the 3rd interval, ♭5 passing tone, and bending/vibrato into one, completing your blues phrasing (BPM 80). Play a 3-bar phrase without a single break, and if you can hum it back with your own voice, it's officially become real "music."
- **Day 4** — The one-month finish line. Record a whole one-chord (Am7) jam (BPM 80), give yourself feedback, and check your own progress against the full month's wrap-up checklist.

## Key Feel (Target Notes & Philosophy)
- **♭5 is a passing tone.** Don't sit on it — slip it through between 3rd intervals and 16th notes to create tension. Let "4-♭5-5" flow out in one breath.
- **Bending isn't "pushing the string" — it's "landing accurately on the target pitch"** — tune it by ear. Vibrato isn't a fingertip tremor, it's an even wrist rotation.
- Build tension with ♭5, but the phrase still ends by landing on the 3rd (C) to resolve it. Tension and resolution — that's the whole story of blues.

## What Changes When You're Done
- You can locate ♭5 across all 5 boxes and weave it in naturally as a passing tone.
- You can land half and full bends accurately on the target pitch, and make your landing notes sing with wrist vibrato.
- You can record a one-chord jam, evaluate yourself (♭5 expression + how solidly you land on the 3rd), and personally check off everything you've achieved this month. If you've made it this far, you're not "the intermediate player who freezes up" anymore. You're ready for next month — real improv where the chords actually change.
