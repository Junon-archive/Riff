# Week 12 — Composing, Recording, and Self-Reviewing an Original Solo (Your 3-Month Graduation)

This is finally the last week. You've built up so much over the past 11 weeks — connecting all five pentatonic boxes, blues ♭5, bending and vibrato, chord-tone targeting, ii-V-I guide tones, Dorian's major 6th, Mixolydian's ♭7, and switching modes by genre. But right now, all of that is still **scattered parts in your head**. This week, you gather those parts and build **one original solo that's entirely yours.** Not a copy — a solo you actually composed. This is the final goal of this whole curriculum, and your graduation exam.

## What We're Building This Week
Combine every part from the last 11 weeks — 3rds, 16th-note sequences, chord-tone targeting, Dorian's ♮6, Mixolydian's ♭7 and the blue note, genre-based mode switching, space and phrasing — into **one original solo.** Go through four stages — design, compose, finish & record, self-feedback & retake — to complete a solo you composed yourself.

## Why Composing, Why Now
Choosing your own notes and singing — not copying — has been this curriculum's goal from start to finish. All the pieces you've learned are still scattered, so this week we converge them into one. The song's 8-bar progression (Am7 | D7 | Am7 | D7 | A7 | D7 | Am7 | Am7) has Dorian and Mixolydian living **side by side at the same time**, so everything you learned about color and switching in W9 through W11 naturally comes together in one song. In particular, C (♭3, Dorian) and C# (major 3rd, Mixolydian) sit exactly one fret apart on the 3rd string (5th → 6th fret), so a small push of one finger becomes a "switch" that changes the entire expression of the song.

## This Week's Journey (Day 1 → Day 4)
- **Day 1 — Design: don't play a single note yet.** The most common mistake when building something bigger is stacking bricks before there's a blueprint. So today, put the guitar down for a moment, decide your 8-bar chord progression (the skeleton) first, and gather your landing map into one hand position. Am7 → C (♭3), D7 → F# (=A Dorian's ♮6 color), A7 → C# (Mixolydian's major 3rd). Once the C ↔ C# switch — a single fret apart on the 3rd string — is in your hand, you're already halfway through this week.
- **Day 2 — Composing: toss a motif out, then answer it back.** A map alone still gets you "note picking," not "music." Toss out a short motif (2–4 notes — say, D–E–C, landing then going quiet), then answer it back with a slight variation, call-and-response style. That silence is what gives weight to the note you just played. Take it further — carry your response into the A7 section and answer with C# instead of C, and now your song has a "story": call in Dorian, response in Mixolydian.
- **Day 3 — Finish and record: turn the pieces into one song.** Connect all your pieces into one continuous 8-bar arc, start to finish. Three principles — draw an arc (start quiet → peak at the bar-5 switch to A7 → come back down, landing on the root), make bar 5 your turning point (the C# plus blue-note bend climax), and always close with a long, vibrato-filled full stop on the root A. Then, for the first time, record the whole thing straight through over the backing track.
- **Day 4 — Self-feedback and retake: operate with your ears.** This is where most people stop — because they can't really listen to their own recording. Slow the playback down (0.75x, 0.5x) to magnify what real-time listening never caught, and use section looping to zero in on the weak spots. Grade yourself against four criteria — 3rd-landing accuracy, use of color notes, vibrato stability, and rhythmic timing — and instead of re-recording the whole thing, focus your retake on just the weakest seam (bars 4 to 5, the C → C# switch plus the blue-note climax). Get just those two bars perfect, and the whole song comes alive.

## The Core Feel to Hold Onto
- **Don't play a single note first — start with the skeleton (progression) and your landing map.** Improvising without a design falls apart.
- **When the chord changes, the 3rd changes too:** Am7 → C (♭3), D7 → F#, A7 → C#. This is the final application of the engine that's run through this entire curriculum.
- **A retake means fixing the weakest seam, not re-recording the whole song.** You don't need any special app — a phone voice memo, whatever you've got, will do. The speed control and section-repeat features built into most players are all you need.

## By the End of This Week — and the Final Period on These Three Months
You'll compose an original solo over an 8-bar progression where Dorian and Mixolydian coexist, landing on each chord's 3rd throughout, and you'll build a solo that sings — not just scale-runs — using motifs, call-and-response, and space. You'll grade your own recording against four criteria, retake the weak spots, and finish with a genuinely better take. The plateaued player who used to just copy, whose hands used to freeze the second a backing track started — that player can now write their own solo and operate on their own playing by ear. This is graduation from your 3-month curriculum. Congratulations — truly.
